Definition of Bullying

One day, in the crowded corridors of an Egyptian school, a mother noticed her child walking, trying to hide himself and looking around himself, fearing any sudden hits. The mother wondered what caused her child to be like this. What this mother witnessed is a reality for many families, and this is just one example of how bullying can silently shape a child’s world.

Bullying is not just a word, but it is a wound that cuts deeply into the lives of several people. For many victims, bullying is a daily torment that leaves them feeling anxious, fearful, and with low self-esteem, ruining their social connections and leading to extreme sadness that may culminate in thoughts of self-harm.

Bullying is much more than mere teasing. It is defined as a harmful and repetitive behavior that involves a real or perceived power imbalance by an individual or a group against a weaker one. That behavior devastates a child’s self-esteem and well-being, which can develop a traumatic experience that will last into adulthood. Bullying can take place in several forms as indicated by Khalil et al. (2021) and Abdel-Whahed et al. (2022), and they are addressed below.

"Bullying is the systematic abuse of power and is defined as aggressive behavior or intentional harm-doing by peers that are carried out repeatedly and involve an imbalance of power"
--Dieter Wolke and Suzet Tanya Lereya

"Cyberbullying is poised to turn into the biggest online concern, already affecting up to 35% of all children"
--Dr. Martyn Wild

Facts and Statistics

Bullying among students

A study established by Abdel-Whahed et al. (2022) indicated that 97.2% of the 752 sampled medical students in Egypt experienced bullying, with being a victim rated at 83.3% and being a bully that was rated at 57.7%. This shows the widespread nature of bullying, which highlights the urgent need to stop this problem.

Bullying forms

A study conducted by Khalil et al. (2021) on 350 adolescent school students in Egypt revealed that physical bullying was reported by 53.1% of students and verbal bullying by 49.8%. Cyberbullying and sexual bullying were reported by 23.2% and 12.1% of students, respectively.

Bullying consequences

Bullying can disrupt education or work environments by making victims suffer from trust issues, low self-esteem, and difficulting forming any healthy relationship, making these individuals always feel fear, embarrassment, depression, and isolation. For instance, physical bullying can lead to severe injuries or wounds like the sixth-grader student who was assaulted by her peers in front of other students (Hashem, 2025).

Bullying consequences

Bullying can lead to suicidal thoughts. BBC News (2022) indicated that a 17-year-old girl suicided because of cyberbullying as she was threatened by AI-edited photos depicting her in a state of nudity.

Our vision

Bullying is a severe problem worldwide that causes several detrimental consequences, affecting their mental health, academic performance, and overall well-being, and it needs to be stopped as soon as possible.

While anti-bullying programs in schools contribute to lessening this problem, the responsibility does not end with schools, as parents can play a crucial role in preventing bullying before it escalates.
