Do Children Mirror Parents' Behaviors?

Have you ever noticed how the way parents handle conflict at home seems to repeat in their children’s behavior at school? As a child, I often noticed how my peers’ parents shaped the way they treated others. I remember one friend whose father, himself a victim of bullying as told by my friend, sometimes responds to conflict with anger or silence.

Another friend’s mother, who had grown up in a supportive home, always encourages open conversations and kindness with all of his friends. What stood out to me was how their children behaved exactly like them. My friend with the aggressive father seemed aggressive too when facing challenges at school. On the other hand, the friend with the calm and supportive mother appeared confident and handled conflicts effectively. So, do children mirror their parents’ behaviors?

By drawing conclusions from these families, I realized that the long-lasting characteristics of a parent’s own childhood, whether pain or compassion, can deeply influence how they raise their children.

Talking specifically about bullying, the effects of experiencing bullying at a young age can last into adulthood and lead future generations to experience bullying too, whether as victims or bullies. Therefore, it is mandatory to break the cycle of intergenerational bullying to ensure effective prevention of bullying behavior in future generations by healing past traumas and cultivating resilience for the future.

The Impact of Unresolved Childhood Experiences on Parenting

Unresolved experiences of being bullied or displaying aggression in a parent’s own childhood can deeply influence their parenting patterns. Research by Al-Hussein and Shahba (2024) highlights that family dynamics such as exposure to violence, lack of emotional support, and poor communication are significant predictors of bullying behavior among teenagers.

When parents carry unhealed wounds or normalized aggression from their own upbringing experience, these patterns can unconsciously appear in their parenting patterns, shaping how they interact with and guide their children (Chen et al., 2020). As a result, their children will be raised on the concepts of these parenting styles including aggression and violence, leading them to exhibit bullying outside the house boundaries and keeping these traits in them that will reflect on the upbringing of their future children. This is the endless cycle of aggression that we need to break.

Pathways to Healing and Breaking Harmful Cycles

Fortunately, healing these patterns is possible. Approaches like counseling, support groups, and resilience training empower these parents to recognize and stop harmful cycles of aggression. For instance, applying Programs that focus on emotional development and resilience involving both children and their adults has shown positive effects in decreasing bullying behavior among school children as parents understood how to mitigate these patterns (Cunha et al., 2023).​

Read More


Moreover, Studies show that when parents develop empathy and effective communication skills, they create a nurturing family environment that discourages bullying (Chen et al., 2020). Parent-directed programs that focus on emotional development decrease bullying rates among their children and promote their social behaviors and emotional well-being. Therefore, addressing parents’ own past traumas can break the repeating of negative behaviors and enhance their ability for healthy conflict resolution and emotional regulation for their children.

Building a Foundation for Generational Change Through Awareness

Parents who experienced bullying themselves may unconsciously perpetuate similar behaviors in their children. However, recognizing these patterns is the first step toward change. Teaching parents how to develop more empathy and resilience equips future generations with the tools to prevent bullying before it starts.

So, by healing past traumas of parents and helping them promote empathy, families can break the cycle of bullying and build a safer and more compassionate future for their children. Take the first step today by exploring parent education resources, seeking support if needed, and making your family a place of kindness and care. Together, we can end the cycle of bullying!
